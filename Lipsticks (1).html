Business Problem

hema is a frequent online shopper who regularly purchases cosmetic products, especially lipsticks, from e-commerce platforms. While browsing lipsticks on Amazon, she notices that prices vary significantly across brands, shades, and product typeseven when the products appear similar in quality and usage.

For similar lipstick products, Ananya observes cases where:

  • One brand offers a lipstick for around 150
  • Another brand prices a similar lipstick above 1,000
  • Some lipsticks with high discounts still receive good customer ratings
  • Certain premium-priced lipsticks have ratings similar to budget products
  • The same brand offers multiple lipsticks at different price points
  • Discount percentages vary widely across products

These observations raise several questions in hemas mind:

  • Why do lipstick prices vary so much across brands?
  • Does a higher price always indicate better quality or higher ratings?
  • Are discounted lipsticks good value for money?
  • Which brands offer the best balance between price and customer satisfaction?
  • How do pricing strategies differ between budget, mid-range, and premium brands?
  • These questions are relevant not only for individual customers like Ananya, but also for cosmetic brands and sellers who compete in a highly saturated and price-sensitive market. Understanding these pricing patterns can help both consumers make informed purchase decisions and businesses optimize their pricing strategies.

Business Problem Statement

Lipstick prices on e-commerce platforms are influenced by multiple factors such as brand positioning, product weight, shade variety, discounts, and customer ratings. This creates uncertainty for customers who want to identify the best value-for-money products and for businesses aiming to understand competitive pricing behavior. To reduce ambiguity and uncover meaningful pricing insights, this project applies Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to:

  • Compare lipstick prices across different brands
  • Analyze the relationship between price, discounts, and customer ratings
  • Identify budget, mid-range, premium, and luxury lipstick segments
  • valuate how discounts impact customer perception
  • Determine brands and products that offer the best value for money

The insights derived from this analysis can support smarter consumer choices and provide actionable business intelligence for cosmetic retailers and brands.

Hypothesis 1 One Way ANOVA

Question: Do average product ratings differ across different price categories?

Null Hypothesis (H0): All price categories have equal mean ratings

Alternate Hypothesis (H1): At least one price category has a different mean rating

In[6]:
from scipy.stats import f_oneway
import seaborn as sns
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

groups = []
for cat in df['Price_Category'].unique():
    groups.append(df[df['Price_Category'] == cat]['Rating'].values)

stat, p_value = f_oneway(*groups)

cl = 0.95
alpha = 1 - cl

if p_value <= alpha:
    print("Reject Null Hypothesis. i.e There is relationship between variables")
else:
    print("Failed to reject Null Hypothesis. i.e There is no relationship between variables")

plt.figure(figsize=(8,4))
sns.boxplot(x='Price_Category', y='Rating', data=df)
plt.title('H1: Rating vs Price Category')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Reject Null Hypothesis. i.e There is relationship between variables

Key Insight : The ANOVA test shows a statistically significant difference in ratings across price categories. However, the boxplot indicates that the differences are small in magnitude, with all categories clustering around similar median ratings. This suggests higher-priced products are rated only slightly better, not dramatically better.

Hypothesis 2 (Chi-Square)

Question: Is shade intensity dependent on price category?

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): Shade intensity is independent of price category

  • Alternate Hypothesis (H1): Shade intensity is dependent on price category

Hypothesis 3 (T-Test)

Question: Does discount level influence product rating?

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): Mean rating is the same for high-discount and low-discount products

  • Alternate Hypothesis (H1): Mean rating differs between high-discount and low-discount products

In[15]:
from scipy.stats import ttest_ind
In[16]:
median_discount = df['Discount_Amount'].median()

df['Discount_Group'] = df['Discount_Amount'].apply(
    lambda x: 'High Discount' if x >= median_discount else 'Low Discount'
)

high = df[df['Discount_Group'] == 'High Discount']['Rating']
low = df[df['Discount_Group'] == 'Low Discount']['Rating']

stat, p_value = ttest_ind(high, low, equal_var=False)

alpha = 1 - cl

if p_value <= alpha:
    print("Reject Null Hypothesis. i.e There is relationship between variables")
else:
    print("Failed to reject Null Hypothesis. i.e There is no relationship between variables")

plt.figure(figsize=(8,4))

Failed to reject Null Hypothesis. i.e There is no relationship between variables

Key Insight : The T-test shows no significant difference in ratings between high-discount and low-discount products. Both groups cluster around similar median ratings in the boxplot, indicating that deeper discounts do not improve customer rating sentiment for lipsticks in this dataset.

Conclusion

What We Observed (Overall Market Behavior)

  • Lipsticks show strong tiering in price (Budget, Mid-Range, Luxury, Premium).
  • Ratings are tightly clustered and skew high, indicating strong consumer satisfaction.
  • Discounts vary widely across products but do not translate to higher ratings.
  • Shade intensities are uniformly distributed across price tiers.
  • MRP and Selling Price follow a proportional pattern, confirming pricing consistency.

Bivariate + Multivariate EDA Key Learnings

1. Rating Distribution

Ratings are highly concentrated between 3.5 and 4.5, with a mean near 4.0.

Interpretation:
Products are generally well-received; negative ratings are rare.

2. Price Category vs Average Selling Price

Premium and Luxury categories command higher selling prices, with clear price stratification.

Interpretation:
Pricing strategy is structured and market-position driven.

3. Discount Amount vs Rating

Scatterplot shows overlapping clusters with no upward trend.

Interpretation:
Discounts drive conversions, not sentiment; reviews remain stable.

4. MRP vs Selling Price

Strong positive relationship observed; higher MRP products sell at higher prices.

Interpretation:
Brands maintain price discipline across tiers.

Hypothesis Validation (EDA Statistics)

EDA insights were validated statistically:

  • H1 (Price Category vs Rating ANOVA): Reject H0 (Significant)
  • H2 (Shade vs Price Category Chi-Square): Fail to Reject H0 (Not Significant)
  • H3 (Discount vs Rating T-Test): Fail to Reject H0 (Not Significant)

Interpretation

Visual patterns were consistent with statistical outcomes.
Although price category affects ratings, the practical effect is modest due to clustered medians.
Shade and discount strategies do not modify customer perception or review behavior.

Final Business Insights

  • Premium pricing does not generate proportionally higher ratings.
  • Consumer perception appears driven by formulation and shade suitability, not pricing.
  • Discounting should be treated as a volume lever, not a sentiment lever.
  • Shade preferences are universal across affordability bands.
  • Pricing and MRP alignment suggests strong brand positioning consistency.

Recommendations

  • Compete on product experience and brand value over aggressive discounting.
  • Use discounts to boost sales volume, not review scores.
  • Expand inclusive shade ranges rather than pricing-based segmentation.
  • Maintain consistent formulation quality across price tiers.
  • Reinforce premium tiers through branding rather than discount manipulation.

Final Conclusion

Lipsticks exhibit structured pricing tiers and high consumer satisfaction across categories.
Statistical testing confirms that price tier impacts ratings, but differences are small in practice.
Shade intensity and discounting do not influence rating sentiment, indicating that cosmetic consumers evaluate based on product performance and shade fit rather than monetization strategies.
